Introduction to Phylum Porifera

  • History: The sponge has a history that dates back over 600 million years and is found in all depths of water.

  • Specialized cells: They are the simplest multicellular creatures; they lack a brain and a heart and muscles; they are composed of special cells such as archaeocytes, amoebocytes, and pinacocytes.

  • Water Canal Systems: These are fed by a filtering of water through a canal system that may be of ascon, sycon, or leucon types with a skeletal support of mineral spicules or spongin.

  • Reproductive capacity: Porifera can reproduce both sexually and asexually and have a remarkably high biological capacity for regeneration of lost or damaged parts.

  • Ecological Symbiosis: These primitive organisms frequently form vital symbiotic associations with other marine life, acting as essential components of their underwater ecosystems.

Sub-phyla Classification

✶ Phylum Porifera (Sponges)

The taxonomy of Phylum Porifera is mainly based on the chemical composition of the skeletal spicules and the general structural framework of the animal, on which four main classes are recognized.

Class Calcarea

  • Skeleton composed of calcium carbonate spicules.
  • Body form may be vase-shaped, thin tubular networks, or irregular and massive.
  • Mostly small-sized sponges.
  • Inhabit shallow waters of all seas, from intertidal zones to about 200 m.
  • Some species occur as deep as 800 m.
  • Approximately 300 species are known.

Class Hexactinellida (Hyalospongiae)

  • Skeleton is distinctively composed of six-rayed siliceous spicules (slilca).
  • They are exclusively marine and typically inhabit deep waters, ranging from 25 to 8,500 m.
  • Most species attach to hard substrates, but some are found in soft sediments.
  • Key examples include Hexactinella, Euplectella (Venus' flower basket), and Farrea.
  • Approximately 500 species are known in this class.

Class Demospongiae

  • Skeleton is typically composed of silica spicules, spongin fibers, or a combination of both; some species entirely lack a skeleton.
  • It is the largest class of sponges; there are about 4,200 species..
  • Exist in a variety of ocean environments, ranging from shallow to very deep water (5,500 m depth).
  • Includes the freshwater sponges (Family Spongillidae).
  • Body size and form are highly variable, ranging from small patches to massive structures over 2 m wide.

Class Homoscleromorpha

  • Skeletal absent or made up of simple siliceous spicules
  • Exclusively marine sponges
  • Mostly found in shallow coastal waters, attached to hard substrates
  • Body form small, thin, encrusting, or cushion-like
  • Have true epithelial tissues with a basement membrane to indicate advanced cellular organization
  • Canal system is leuconoid
  • Comprises approximately 100 species

Key Features

  • No true tissues or organs — only cells
  • Asymmetrical body but some are radially symmetrical
  • Mostly marine but some are freshwater (Spongilla)
  • Body surface contains numerous minute pores called ostia and few large pores called osculum.
  • Respire through general body surface
  • Reproduce asexually by budding and regeneration, but sexually by gametes
  • They are sessile, and mode of nutrition is holozoic

The Water Canal System

Poriferans are nature’s water pumps. Water flows through ostia (tiny pores), travels through canals and chambers powered by choanocytes, and exits through a larger opening called the osculum.

Specialized Cells

  • Choanocytes — have tiny whip-like tails to move water and catch food
  • Amoebocytes — carry nutrients around and help build the skeleton
  • Pinacocytes — flat cells that form the outer layer and protect the sponge

Body Plan Types

  • Asconoid — simplest tube-like design
  • Syconoid — folded walls increase surface area
  • Leuconoid — most complex, very efficient filtering

Reproduction

  • Asexual — budding, fragmentation, gemmules
  • Sexual — most are hermaphrodites; produce swimming larvae

Diversity Summary

  • Calcarea — lime sponges (e.g., Sycon)
  • Hexactinellida — glass sponges with delicate silica skeletons
  • Demospongiae — the largest group of sponges; includes marine and freshwater species (like bath sponges).
  • Homoscleromorpha — small group of marine sponges; known for having simple siliceous spicules, leuconoid canal systems, and unique true tissues with a basement membrane.

Ecological and Economic Importance

  • Filter water and help maintain clean marine ecosystems
  • Provide habitats and shelter for small marine animals
  • Some species are harvested for commercial use (e.g., bath sponges)
  • Are a potential source of unique bioactive chemical compounds for new medicines
